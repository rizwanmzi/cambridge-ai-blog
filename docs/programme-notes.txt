-- 1 of 31 --

Monday 2 March
Machine Learning
Visual 1. Reconstructed spectrum used to explain why simpler models are easier to govern and more complex models are usually
more accurate.
Context and framing
ï‚· Machine learning was treated as foundational rather than obsolete. The fact that it is no longer
the newest part of the AI conversation does not make it less commercially useful.
ï‚· A recurring framing point was proportionality. Many business problems do not need the latest
model family. Some can be solved with simple rules, averages or threshold logic; others need
machine learning; only a subset genuinely need generative or agentic systems.
ï‚· The discussion started from everyday examples rather than theory. Recommendation systems,
spending forecasts, inbox ranking, fraud monitoring, image classification and journey-time
prediction were used to show how deeply predictive systems already shape behaviour.
ï‚· A deliberate distinction was drawn between narrow predictive systems and broader generative
systems. Predictive systems can be very powerful, but only inside the task they were trained to
perform.
Key themes explored
ï‚· Prediction versus explanation was the central conceptual divide. Traditional statistics was framed
as a tool for understanding why the world works as it does, while machine learning was framed
as a tool for predicting outcomes with as much accuracy as possible.
ï‚· Model choice was presented as a governance issue, not merely a technical one. Organisations do
not have to accept a black box by default. Leadership can ask for a more interpretable system
when explanation is necessary for regulators, executives or customers.

-- 2 of 31 --

ï‚· The world is often too complex to hand-specify neatly. A workshop exercise on employee attrition
quickly produced large numbers of interacting variables, non-linear relationships and contextual
dependencies, illustrating why machine learning becomes attractive when explicit modelling
becomes unwieldy.
ï‚· There was strong emphasis on the idea that machine learning is not one thing. Different
algorithms create different trade-offs around interpretability, stability, data hunger and
predictive power.
ï‚· The session also highlighted that machine learning still outperforms broader generative systems
for many narrow tasks. Weather forecasting, credit risk, some fraud patterns and many
classification tasks remain classic predictive problems.
ï‚· Sentiment analysis was used to show how the boundary has shifted. Historically it sat squarely in
machine learning. In practice, newer large models often now solve it more easily because they
cope better with the ambiguity of natural language.
Technical or conceptual clarifications
ï‚· The baseline frame was supervised learning: a dataset, a known outcome, and an algorithm that
learns a mapping from inputs to outputs.
ï‚· Holdout testing and cross-validation were presented as the real test of model quality. The aim is
not to explain the past elegantly, but to predict unseen cases reliably.
ï‚· Linear regression was discussed as the baseline model. Its advantages were mathematical
simplicity, replicability, low data requirements and a clear causal-looking story. Its weaknesses
were an inability to represent complex interactions and non-linear relationships without manual
intervention.
ï‚· Logistic regression was positioned as the probability-safe version of regression, especially useful
where outputs must sit cleanly between zero and one.
ï‚· Decision trees were used to illustrate explainable branching logic. They remain readable, but can
become unwieldy when many layers are added.
ï‚· Random forests were explained as ensembles of small trees. Averaging many weak learners
improves accuracy, but makes the final answer harder to narrate in human terms.
ï‚· Neural networks were described as stacked combinations of simple functions, conceptually
similar to linking large numbers of logistic-style units together. Their strength is that they learn
features and interactions from the data rather than relying on human theories about what
matters.
ï‚· Backpropagation and training randomness were discussed in practical terms. Weight
initialisation, training order and architecture choice all affect whether a network converges
cleanly or gets stuck. This is one reason model building feels experimental rather than fully
deterministic in practice.
ï‚· Two practical explainability workarounds were covered. One is sensitivity testing, changing a
single variable to see whether the output changes. The other is retraining a model with or without
a feature to see whether its inclusion materially changes predictions.
Points of agreement
ï‚· Simple methods should be preferred when they are adequate.

-- 3 of 31 --

ï‚· Performance on unseen data is the decisive test of predictive usefulness.
ï‚· Explainability matters more in high-stakes, regulated or politically sensitive settings.
ï‚· Neural networks and other black-box methods are not inevitable. The trade-off can be chosen.
Areas of debate or nuance
ï‚· The line between machine learning and AI was recognised as fuzzy in practice, especially where a
workflow mixes predictive models and large models.
ï‚· Machine learning errors and generative hallucinations were treated as different phenomena.
Predictive models misclassify or miss patterns; generative systems can invent plausible but
unfounded content.
ï‚· Humans are often granted more tolerance for error than machines. That social difference matters
when considering self-driving cars, automated decisions or regulatory oversight.
ï‚· There was useful scepticism about explanations generated after the fact by humans or LLMs. A
neat explanation is not necessarily a faithful description of the process that produced the answer.
Notable observations and examples
ï‚· The Netflix prize was used as an example of how benchmarking accelerated progress. Teams
competed for a ten per cent improvement, and the winning result emerged through model
combinations and intense iteration.
ï‚· Examples used in the room included loan scoring, fraud detection, medical image classification,
recommendation systems, sentiment analysis, music generation and code generation.
ï‚· The employee attrition exercise surfaced a rich list of variables: age, tenure, training, pay
progression, engagement signals, market demand for skills, AI adoption, family stability needs,
personal wealth changes and combinations of these variables rather than any one factor alone.
ï‚· The neural-network playground exercise made the practical messiness of training explicit. Bigger
networks learned more quickly, but randomness mattered, data preparation mattered, and there
was no elegant closed-form way to know the right architecture in advance.

-- 4 of 31 --

Cloud Computing and AI: from infrastructure to enterprise capability
Visual 2. Reconstructed cloud stack showing how infrastructure, data, platforms and applications were treated as distinct but
interdependent layers.
Context and framing
ï‚· Cloud was framed as the enabling substrate of the current AI moment, analogous to plugging
organisations into an electricity grid rather than generating everything themselves.
ï‚· The original appeal of cloud was the move from capital expenditure to operating expenditure.
That basic shift still matters, but the discussion now sits at a different scale because AI workloads
make infrastructure scarcity strategic.
ï‚· A repeated phrase was that organisations want breakthrough without breakdown. They want
autonomous, high-performing systems without losing resilience, control or trust.
Key themes explored
ï‚· Infrastructure is back in the strategy conversation. Data-centre capacity, chip availability, energy
and network design are no longer invisible background matters. They shape what can be built
and how quickly.
ï‚· AI has changed the economics of the stack. Inference and training place different demands on
infrastructure than earlier waves of enterprise digitisation.
ï‚· The data layer was repeatedly treated as the true long-term differentiator. Models are moving
towards commodity status. Proprietary data, clean pipelines and usable governance are harder to
replicate.
ï‚· Provider choice matters less at the level of marketing claims and more at the level of strategic fit,
legacy estate, internal skills and the type of workloads being prioritised.

-- 5 of 31 --

ï‚· Citizen development was treated as a structural consequence of natural-language interfaces. More
people can now build useful applications, but this pushes organisations towards new governance
models rather than old-style central control.
ï‚· Shadow AI was treated as a predictable response to friction. If official tools are absent or approval
processes are too slow, employees will bring in their own tools and data will leave the
organisation in unmanaged ways.
ï‚· Digital sovereignty and jurisdictional fragmentation were highlighted as a real design variable,
especially for organisations exposed to European, American and Chinese legal environments.
Technical or conceptual clarifications
ï‚· The session distinguished general-purpose processors, graphics processors and tensor-oriented
processors as different layers of compute suited to different kinds of workload.
ï‚· The stack itself was broken into four layers: infrastructure and compute; data and pipelines; AI
and ML platforms; and application or agent layers.
ï‚· The data layer was described historically: relational databases for structured transactions, then
warehouses and lakes for wider analytics, then richer pipelines to support modern AI workflows.
ï‚· ETL and data engineering were treated as persistent essentials. AI does not remove the need to
extract, transform, clean and govern data.
ï‚· AI platforms were described as standardised environments for training, tuning, deploying and
monitoring models rather than rebuilding the full toolchain from scratch each time.
ï‚· MLOps remained important even in a generative era. Models still need to be monitored for drift,
bias, degradation and deployment reliability.
ï‚· Reserved or provisioned throughput was mentioned as a practical response to capacity scarcity.
Not all users now sit in the same queue for access to leading models or services.
Points of agreement
ï‚· Cloud strategy now materially affects AI strategy.
ï‚· Garbage in, garbage out remains completely true in the generative era.
ï‚· Guardrails are more realistic than gates when experimentation is already distributed across the
workforce.
ï‚· A single source of truth across functions is necessary if AI-enabled workflows are to produce
consistent value.
Areas of debate or nuance
ï‚· Build versus platform remained context-specific. Speed, control, future lock-in and available
talent pull the decision in different directions.
ï‚· It was not taken for granted that data should always move to the cloud. In some settings it may
make more sense to bring compute to the data.
ï‚· Provider differences were treated as real but often overstated. In many cases all major providers
can do the job. What matters is where a specific nuance becomes strategically relevant.
ï‚· Switching providers was described as possible but non-trivial, with most growth still coming from
new workloads rather than wholesale migration.

-- 6 of 31 --

Notable observations and examples
ï‚· Investment by major providers was described as rising from earlier large-scale spending to
something closer to a half-trillion-dollar annual commitment in the AI era.
ï‚· A simple but powerful analogy compared AI to the shipping container. The first-order effect is
process efficiency; the second-order effect is redesign of the whole surrounding system because
coordination costs drop.
ï‚· Guardrails were broken down into deterministic rails, semantic rails and economic rails,
reinforcing the idea that governance is not one mechanism but a layered design problem.
ï‚· Showback and chargeback models were discussed as ways of making costs visible and disciplining
experimentation without shutting it down completely.
ï‚· Sovereignty models differed across providers, but the larger point was that local legal structures
increasingly shape architecture and vendor choices.
Decentralised Machine Learning
Context and framing
ï‚· The session began by contrasting natural swarms with choreographed drone displays. The
underlying question was whether intelligent coordination requires a central controller.
ï‚· Collective intelligence was treated as both an engineering question and a governance question.
Concentrating data and control in a few hands may be efficient, but it changes resilience,
ownership and power.
ï‚· The argument was not that decentralisation is always superior. The argument was that it changes
the trade-offs in ways that are increasingly relevant for privacy, sustainability and robustness.
Key themes explored
ï‚· Four architectural patterns were compared: centralised, local, federated and decentralised.
ï‚· The big design variables were where data sits, who controls the model, who performs the
computation, who sees the updates, and how information moves across the system.
ï‚· Nature was used as a recurring source of design insight. Local interaction can produce collective
behaviour that is fluid, resilient and difficult to attack because there is no single point of failure.
ï‚· Scaling current monolithic approaches was challenged as potentially unsustainable. Compute
demand appears to be rising faster than hardware capability and energy systems can support
over long periods.
ï‚· Model compositionality and distillation were proposed as more promising directions than
continual brute-force scaling in some domains.
ï‚· Consensus was presented as a core decentralised protocol: a way for a group to converge on a
shared state through local exchange rather than central command.
Technical or conceptual clarifications
ï‚· Centralised systems place data, model parameters and compute in the cloud. This is efficient and
highly controllable, but creates ownership concentration and a single strategic choke point.
ï‚· Local systems keep data, model and compute on the device. This strengthens privacy and reduces
latency, but weakens generalisation because each model only sees a narrow slice of reality.

-- 7 of 31 --

ï‚· Federated systems keep raw data local but send updates to a central model. This reduces data
movement, but does not eliminate privacy risk because updates themselves can reveal
information.
ï‚· Decentralised systems keep data and parameters local and allow neighbours to exchange
selectively. This lowers central authority but slows convergence and shifts responsibility back to
the edge.
ï‚· Foundation models were described as large pre-trained models that generalise broadly. In
robotics, the challenge is that many of them are still too slow and too heavy to run in real time on-
board.
ï‚· Inference frequency matters in embodied systems. A flying system needs decisions far faster than
many large models can currently support on local hardware.
ï‚· Consensus was demonstrated through a classroom averaging exercise. Even without everyone
speaking to everyone else, the room could converge on a common value through repeated local
exchange.
ï‚· A corrupted or malicious node can distort the whole consensus if the system lacks defences. This
is why trust, filtering and redundant connections matter in decentralised settings.
Points of agreement
ï‚· Centralised systems are easier to control and optimise.
ï‚· Federated learning is not a full privacy answer.
ï‚· Single points of failure matter more as systems become more consequential.
ï‚· Compute, power and hardware constraints are becoming strategic rather than purely technical
issues.
Areas of debate or nuance
ï‚· Reduced privacy risk is not the same as full privacy. Information is still moving in both federated
and decentralised systems.
ï‚· Control and resilience pull in different directions. Removing the centre improves fault tolerance
but complicates supervision.
ï‚· It remains uncertain how quickly hardware or new paradigms such as quantum computing will
change the current resource trajectory.
ï‚· The meaning of decentralisation becomes more complex once agents are no longer aligned by
design and may begin to compete or manipulate one another.
Notable observations and examples
ï‚· The traffic demonstration was memorable because the system-level gain came from very small
local sacrifices. Slight slowing by one lane allowed smoother merging and substantially better
throughput.
ï‚· A collaborative mapping example showed robots building local maps and sharing updates until
the collective estimate approximated the ground truth without a central mapping authority.
ï‚· The sustainability argument was quantitative as well as conceptual. Model training demands
were described as rising far faster than the historical pace of underlying hardware improvement.
ï‚· The proposed alternative was smaller, reusable models that can be composed and distilled,
creating combinatorial capability without always retraining giant monoliths from scratch.

Tuesday 3 March
Generative AI: from hype to business impact
Visual 3. Reconstructed continuum used to distinguish predictive, generative, agentic and physical AI, and to highlight why
governance changes when systems move from answering to acting.
Context and framing
ï‚· The session focused on how to think about generative AI as a business capability rather than as a
sequence of viral demos.
ï‚· A strong early point was that adoption cycles are compressing. Getting onto the road, learning
quickly and pivoting fast may matter more than waiting for mature certainty.
ï‚· Participants were encouraged to understand how the underlying mechanism works, because that
makes it easier to interpret successive waves of tools rather than treating each new release as
wholly novel.
Key themes explored
ï‚· Open versus closed models was a central theme. Closed systems were once assumed to hold a
decisive quality lead, but open-weight and open-source alternatives were described as closing the
gap quickly.
ï‚· The market was treated as diversifying rapidly rather than consolidating around one provider.
The implication was that model choice alone is unlikely to remain a durable competitive moat.
ï‚· Generative AI was shown to be moving across modalities. Images, music, code, text and voice
were all discussed as active fronts of development and commercial experimentation.
ï‚· Practical value depends heavily on context and orchestration. The raw model is generic. The
enterprise system around it determines whether it is useful.

-- 10 of 31 --

ï‚· Generative tools were framed as especially good at accelerating boring, time-consuming, low-
differentiation work in knowledge work and development.
ï‚· Another theme was the short life cycle of specific use cases. The value may sit less in any one
application and more in building the organisational habit of ongoing experimentation.
Technical or conceptual clarifications
ï‚· Large language models were described as probabilistic next-word systems operating over the
prompt context and whatever is known about the user or task context.
ï‚· Training corpora were discussed explicitly. Large models draw on extremely large web-based
corpora that can include encyclopaedic material, forums, books and sometimes contested or
pirated content.
ï‚· Versioning often involves starting again at greater scale rather than simply extending a previous
model in place.
ï‚· Data poisoning and falsehood injection were raised as real risks. If false material enters the
training corpus at scale, it can surface later as convincing but incorrect output.
ï‚· Context windows were used to explain why models can appear more capable when given more
material, but also why only a bounded amount of context can be retained at inference time.
ï‚· Temperature was described as a practical control on randomness. Small parameter changes can
lead to large output differences.
ï‚· Emergence was defined as the appearance of capabilities not explicitly designed in by the
developer, but arising as scale and complexity increase.
ï‚· Fine-tuning and persona overlays were discussed as ways of imposing more morality, style or
behavioural consistency onto models whose base training reflects a broad and sometimes messy
internet distribution.
ï‚· Retrieval-augmented generation was broken into three parts: retrieve relevant knowledge from a
store, integrate it into the model context, and generate an answer grounded partly in that
retrieved material.
Points of agreement
ï‚· Generative models out of the box are not built around any one organisationâ€™s process.
ï‚· Agility matters more than fixed-state design because the pace of change is too fast for static plans.
ï‚· Oversight is still necessary. Human error has always existed, and AI error now needs comparable
training and governance responses.
ï‚· Prompt quality, context quality and system design often matter more than headline model
branding.
Areas of debate or nuance
ï‚· Open models were seen as both a strategic opportunity and a governance concern, especially
where competition, security or geopolitics matter.
ï‚· Emergence was treated cautiously. It may unlock useful capability, but it also complicates
forecasting and safety assurance.
ï‚· There was no settled answer on how much fine-tuned morality or personality should be layered
onto a system trained on broad internet data.

-- 11 of 31 --

ï‚· The degree to which generative systems should be treated as creativity tools, simulation tools or
workflow infrastructure remained deliberately open.
Notable observations and examples
ï‚· The adoption comparison to earlier platforms was used to underline the speed of diffusion.
Generative tools were framed as reaching mass usage far faster than earlier consumer platforms.
ï‚· Examples covered synthetic imagery, music generation, voice cloning for accessibility uses, and
AI-assisted advertising and translation.
ï‚· A recurring warning was that chasing the best model is a false economy. The more durable
advantage lies in the organisational context wrapped around the model.
ï‚· The OODA cycle was introduced as a way of structuring how firms can observe, orient, decide and
act in a rapidly changing model landscape.
ï‚· The session ended by bridging into a broader AI continuum, including predictive, generative,
agentic, physical and explainable AI, making clear that governance needs change as systems gain
agency.
AI tools, organisational adoption and leadership capacity
Context and framing
ï‚· This discussion moved from model mechanics to organisational behaviour: how firms actually
adopt tools, what blocks value, and what leaders need to do differently.
ï‚· The dominant message was that AI should be understood as a strategic capability rather than as a
race to licence whichever model is newest.
Key themes explored
ï‚· Core tools such as general assistants, copilots and specialist legal or research tools were seen as
becoming baseline capabilities rather than differentiators in themselves.
ï‚· The absence of approved tools was described as a bigger risk than the presence of imperfect ones
because it drives unmanaged shadow AI behaviour.
ï‚· Problem-driven adoption was clearly preferred to technology-driven roll-out. People adopt faster
when a tool solves a recognised business problem rather than arriving as an abstract mandate.
ï‚· The best use of AI was described as sparking thinking, not outsourcing it. A deliberately
adversarial or challenging agent can improve judgement rather than replace it.
ï‚· Organisations were urged to move beyond incremental add-ons. The deeper opportunity is to
rewire workflows, roles and information flows.
Technical or conceptual clarifications
ï‚· The OODA model was used as a simple strategic framework. Observe what is changing, orient to
what that means locally, decide how to respond, and act quickly enough to learn.
ï‚· Productivity gains were discussed in pragmatic terms. Summarisation, preparation, reference
retrieval and tool-guided drafting can release meaningful leadership capacity even if they do not
transform the whole firm.
ï‚· Tool versatility was seen as a double-edged sword. General-purpose AI tools can do many things,
but without training and examples they are often underused.

-- 12 of 31 --

Points of agreement
ï‚· Model providers will commoditise faster than many firms expect.
ï‚· Visible internal use cases and safe spaces for experimentation are essential.
ï‚· Leadership messaging matters because employees need permission to learn in public.
ï‚· There remains a strong place for human judgement, especially where emotion, trust or major life
decisions are involved.
Areas of debate or nuance
ï‚· There was tension between control and enablement in IT functions. Easy app creation can feel
liberating or destabilising depending on the operating model.
ï‚· Not all customer journeys should be flattened into digital self-service. In some areas, including
major financial decisions, people still value human interaction as part of the service itself.
ï‚· There was discomfort about over-outsourcing judgement, captured in the idea of metacognitive
laziness or AI obesity.
Notable observations and examples
ï‚· Research and finance agents were described as already useful for sifting large information
volumes and running end-to-end processes that would otherwise consume staff time.
ï‚· One warning centred on AI-generated public commentary that sounds superficially correct but
creates reputational risk because it is visibly synthetic or derivative.
ï‚· Another thread argued that job design may invert over time, with empathy, customer interaction
and critical thinking becoming more central in role descriptions than some technical tasks now
are.
Agentic AI foundations and a live HR case
Visual 4. Reconstructed capability set used to distinguish true agents from simple chat interfaces.

-- 13 of 31 --

Context and framing
ï‚· The session treated agents as a distinct step beyond chat interfaces because they can plan, interact
with tools, sequence actions and in some cases coordinate with other agents.
ï‚· A live handbook-based HR case was used to test both the promise and the limits of early
enterprise agents.
Key themes explored
ï‚· Six features were used to define agentic behaviour: autonomy, goal orientation, environmental
interaction, learning or adaptation, workflow optimisation, and multi-agent communication.
ï‚· The session stressed that poor inputs become more dangerous once systems can act. Bad data does
not merely create bad answers; it can scale bad decisions faster.
ï‚· The HR case showed that a large share of routine questions can be answered from a handbook or
policy corpus, potentially freeing HR staff for coaching, frontline support and more complex work.
ï‚· At the same time, the case exposed the trust problem. Employees may value a safe channel for
sensitive questions, but may also fear that logs, voice data or transcripts could later be used
against them.
ï‚· Consistency, speed and analytics were major value themes. Even when an agent is not perfect, it
can provide standardised answers and surface recurring areas of friction.
Technical or conceptual clarifications
ï‚· True learning was distinguished from the illusion of conversational memory. A model may look as
if it is learning when in fact it is just keeping context inside the thread.
ï‚· More durable learning in a narrow enterprise context may require fine-tuning or persistent
memory design, which increases overhead and governance responsibility.
ï‚· Agent maturity was treated as staged rather than binary. Many current enterprise tools exhibit
only fragments of the full agentic stack.
ï‚· A handbook-based agent was deliberately gamified and stress-tested with emotional, sensitive and
manipulative questions in order to reveal weaknesses.
Points of agreement
ï‚· Agent scope must be explicitly defined before deployment.
ï‚· Red-teaming and deliberate stress testing are necessary because users will quickly probe the
boundaries.
ï‚· Routine questions are the most defensible early terrain for agents.
ï‚· Governance must match the consequences of a bad decision rather than the novelty of the tool.
Areas of debate or nuance
ï‚· There was real disagreement over how far HR and similarly sensitive functions should be
automated, even partially.
ï‚· The trade-off between personalisation and trust remained unresolved. Personalised guidance can
be useful, but can also feel like surveillance.
ï‚· It was not assumed that agents reduce cost immediately. Early versions can add cost if they create
new monitoring, correction and escalation burdens.

-- 14 of 31 --

Notable observations and examples
ï‚· The case highlighted secondary value beyond answering questions: identifying common query
types, spotting confusion clusters, tracking behavioural patterns and surfacing where policy
language may be unclear.
ï‚· A governance matrix linked risk and decision complexity to different operating modes, from
independent automation in low-risk repetitive work to human-only decision making in high-risk
ambiguous work.
ï‚· A three-stage pathway was proposed for organisational evolution: human with assistant, human-
agent teams, and then more agentic teams where digital colleagues own bounded pieces of work.
ï‚· A ten-part responsible AI framework surfaced alongside the case, covering privacy, data ethics,
inclusiveness, accountability, explainability, continual learning, collaboration, legal compliance,
social impact and sustainability.

Professional reflections
Context and framing
ï‚· The end-of-day notes captured a reflective rather than declarative mode. The emphasis was on
what to test next, what seemed to matter most, and how the week was changing the shape of
future work.
Key themes explored
ï‚· Tool experimentation remained central, especially around research and scanning tools.
ï‚· Personality, validation and review came through as recurring topics requiring deeper research
rather than quick conclusions.
ï‚· There was interest in the buy, build or borrow decision for AI capability and in how an ethical
framework changes those choices.
ï‚· The overall mood was that the material was both intellectually energising and destabilising at the
same time.
Notable observations and intellectual threads
ï‚· Direct prompting was noted as a potentially useful coordination technique, not just an individual

ï‚· The reflections suggest a move away from passive observation towards more deliberate, hands-on

ï‚· The final emotional note was not triumphalist. It captured both excitement and concern, which
felt representative of the tone of the week as a whole.

Wednesday 4 March
Synthetic customers, simulated personas and marketing research
Context and framing
ï‚· This session explored agentic AI as a research instrument for simulating customer groups,
generating personas and testing messaging without relying only on conventional sampled focus
groups.
Key themes explored
ï‚· The system was designed to build representative personas from public, survey and aggregated
behavioural data rather than from a tiny hand-built sample.
ï‚· Avoiding drift towards the mean was a major design goal. The intention was to preserve the
diversity of customer viewpoints rather than collapse everything into an average respondent.
ï‚· A further aim was to simulate conversational depth. Users could interact with personas directly,
including in group-style conversations, rather than simply reading static demographic profiles.
ï‚· The discussion repeatedly returned to validation. A simulated population is only useful if it can
predict or reproduce known patterns when tested against holdout data.
Technical or conceptual clarifications
ï‚· Brand research could start from a website, public information or other seed material and produce
brand DNA, target personas, likely channels and early messaging themes.
ï‚· Multiple model families and specialised sub-agents were used to reduce over-reliance on a single
model and to produce more varied outputs.
ï‚· Persona generation was framed as granular enough to support approximate location and segment
clustering, but still probabilistic rather than certain.
ï‚· Conversational personas could respond even where a given answer was not explicitly stored in
the data, because the model inferred a likely response from the wider profile.
ï‚· Validation involved giving the system withheld data and checking whether it could predict or
classify known facts, then refining where it failed.
Points of agreement

Legal and psychological frontiers of AI
Context and framing
ï‚· This session framed AI law as inseparable from questions of value, ownership, uncertainty and
behavioural influence. The discussion moved between copyright, privacy, targeting and the
psychology of interacting with machine systems.
Key themes explored
ï‚· Who owns value created through AI, who gets to observe and predict behaviour, and who draws
the governance boundary were treated as the core frontier questions.
ï‚· Training inputs and outputs were separated analytically. The law may look different depending
on whether the issue is what the model consumed, what it produced, or how a human used it in
collaboration.
ï‚· Style imitation was treated as a particularly awkward zone because copyright protects expression
more clearly than style, while models are often very good at reproducing stylistic signatures.
ï‚· The session also covered privacy breaches, model memorisation, right-to-be-forgotten tensions
and the use of AI systems as persuasive or emotionally engaging intermediaries.
Technical or conceptual clarifications
ï‚· Publicly available material does not automatically become public domain material.
ï‚· A model making something similar is not necessarily the same as a model storing and
reproducing a literal copy. The distinction matters for both copyright and privacy analysis.
ï‚· Transformative-use arguments remain important, but so does the economic substitution question.
If people consume AI output instead of paying the original creator, that market effect changes the
legal analysis.
ï‚· Pure AI output faces uncertain authorship protection in some jurisdictions. Minimal prompting
alone may not be enough to support a strong ownership claim.
ï‚· Human collaborative use may create a stronger authorship argument, but not automatically. The
threshold of meaningful human contribution remains unsettled.
ï‚· Privacy risk can arise even when direct copying was never intended, because models can leak,
infer or recombine personal information in ways users did not foresee.
Points of agreement
ï‚· The legal terrain is moving, fragmented and still uncertain.
ï‚· Businesses cannot assume that current legal ambiguity will protect them indefinitely.
ï‚· Law and psychology interact. People care not just about formal consent but about whether a use
feels fair, visible and understandable.
Areas of debate or nuance
ï‚· There was no stable answer on where transformation becomes infringement, especially at
training scale.

-- 21 of 31 --

ï‚· The room held a tension between protecting creative markets and avoiding a legal regime so
restrictive that it entrenches large incumbents.
ï‚· The degree to which style should be legally protected remained contentious, precisely because
style is central to how many creators create value.
Notable observations and examples
ï‚· The discussion referenced disputes over training on books and images, questions around whether
a model creates a copy or a transformed representation, and examples where AI-generated

ï‚· Psychology re-entered the discussion through the idea that AI is moving from tool to colleague in
some settings, and that empathy, behaviour, persuasion and manipulation therefore become
governance topics rather than just product features.

Context and framing
ï‚· The session asked what it means to call an AI system good, and whether that question can be

Key themes explored
